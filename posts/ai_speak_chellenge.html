Predicting ARKit-52 Blendshapes from Audio

An ensemble, a face refiner, and a blink module walk into a competition.

Introduction

A few weeks ago, our team placed second at the AI-SPEAK competition finals at the Science and Technology Park in Novi Sad. The task: take a raw audio waveform and produce a sequence of 52 facial blendshape coefficients per frame, accurate enough to drive a virtual avatar whose face moves with the synthesized speech. Real-time. No cheating with text. No manual intervention.

This post is not a war story. It is a breakdown of the three things that actually moved the needle for us: a two-model ensemble, a learned face refiner, and a deterministic blink module. None of these are revolutionary on their own. The interesting part is how they compose, and where each one fails if you remove it.

01 · Ensemble: two models with different inductive biases, averaged in raw blendshape space. 02 · Refiner: a small learned linear corrector that restores the expressivity the main loss flattens. 03 · Blink module: deterministic cosine pulses that supply behavior the audio cannot. 04 · Final validation MAE: 0.01844, with each component contributing visible perceptual gains.

The Problem, Briefly

Modern facial animation rigs use blendshapes: a fixed set of canonical face poses (jaw open, mouth funnel, brow furrow, left eye blink, and so on) that get linearly combined per frame to produce continuous expression. Apple's ARKit-52 standard defines 52 of these and has become the de facto interface for avatar animation across the industry.

The job of an audio-driven facial animation system is to predict, for every frame, the value of each of those 52 coefficients given only the audio signal. At 60 FPS this means 60 vectors of 52 floats per second, each in [0, 1], and the result has to look like a face speaking, not a mannequin twitching.

Three things make this harder than it sounds:

  • The mapping from phonemes to face shapes is one-to-many. The same vowel can be pronounced with very different facial expressions depending on emotion, emphasis, and speaker idiosyncrasies.
  • Real-time use cases impose a latency budget. You cannot wait for two seconds of future context before predicting the current frame.
  • The upper face is chronically underactivated in naive models. Brows, eyelids, and nose wrinkles barely move because the loss is dominated by mouth-region coefficients, which carry most of the variance during speech.

Each of the three components I am about to walk through targets one of these.

The Dataset

The competition organizers provided a dataset built specifically for this task: two speakers, spk08 and spk14, with 358 natural recordings and 353 synthetic ones, totaling around 700 audio clips. Each clip ships with frame-aligned ARKit-52 annotations at 60 FPS and phoneme labels covering a vocabulary of 34 phonemes.

We split 85/15 with a fixed seed (1337) for reproducibility, ending up with 304 training clips and 54 validation clips. The split was random over recordings, not over speakers, so both speakers appear in both folds. This matters because models can quietly overfit to a single speaker's prosody when the validation set is held out by speaker.

If you plot mean activity per blendshape, the picture is brutally clear. jawOpen, mouthFunnel, and mouthPucker dominate. Half of the upper-face coefficients are essentially zero across the entire training set. Any naive MSE loss will learn to predict near-zero for those, and you will end up with a frozen forehead.

Architecture in One Paragraph

The encoder takes a log-mel spectrogram (80 bins) plus delta and delta-delta coefficients, giving 240-dim audio features at 60 FPS. A small speaker embedding (dim 16, lookup table over the two speaker IDs) is concatenated at every timestep. Optionally, a character-level text branch with multi-head attention conditions the encoder on the transcript when it is available. The result feeds a temporal encoder, which is either a causal TCN or a bidirectional GRU. Two output heads sit on top: a regression head for the 52 blendshapes and a classification head for the auxiliary phoneme task.

Loss is a multitask sum: smoothed L1 on the blendshapes, a temporal smoothness term, an activity-weighted peak loss that upweights the high-variance mouth coefficients, and the phoneme cross-entropy. The phoneme head exists purely for representation regularization. We never use its output at inference.

So far, nothing exotic. The interesting decisions are downstream.

1. Why Two Models, Not One

We trained two checkpoints to convergence and kept both. They differ in exactly one place: the temporal encoder.

  • baseline_full_v1 uses a causal TCN. Strict causality, robust, fast, good at local patterns.
  • hybrid_bgru_v1 uses a bidirectional GRU, two layers, hidden size 128 per direction. Sees future context, integrates the optional text branch, better at long-range dependencies.

On their own, they post very similar validation MAE numbers. The TCN slightly edges out the BiGRU on overall MAE (0.01895 vs 0.02036), but the BiGRU has lower RMSE (0.04482 vs 0.04514), meaning it makes fewer large mistakes even if it accumulates more small ones. They are not the same model with different seeds. They make different errors.

If two models with different inductive biases make uncorrelated errors on the same validation set, averaging their predictions is essentially free accuracy. You are not training a third model. You are using the variance reduction that comes from disagreement.

The actual ensemble is a weighted linear average in raw blendshape space. No extra training, no learned mixer: 

def ensemble_predict(audio, weights=(0.6, 0.4)):
    pred_tcn = baseline_full_v1(audio)        # (T, 52)
    pred_bgru = hybrid_bgru_v1(audio)         # (T, 52)
    return weights[0] * pred_tcn + weights[1] * pred_bgru

The 0.6 / 0.4 split came out of a small grid search on the validation set. Equal weighting was close, but the TCN's more conservative predictions carried slightly more useful signal. We did not regularize the weights or learn them end-to-end. With 54 validation clips, more sophistication would have been overfitting to noise.

The ensemble alone improves overall MAE to roughly 0.0186, ahead of either standalone model. The bigger story, though, is qualitative: the ensemble produces visibly smoother trajectories on hard frames where one model spikes and the other does not. Variance reduction in the literal sense.

2. The Learned Face Refiner

The ensemble fixed the gross errors. It did not fix the underactivation problem. Brows still barely moved. Blinks were timid. The nose region was effectively frozen. This is the standard failure mode of any MAE-trained model on this kind of imbalanced data: the loss surface rewards predicting near-zero for low-variance coefficients, because the cost of being wrong is small.

You can fix this with retraining tricks (heavier weights on upper-face coefficients, balanced sampling, focal-style losses), but those tend to hurt the well-behaved coefficients in the process. Instead, we kept the main model stable and added a tiny post-processing module that nudges the output toward more expressive territory after the fact.

The face refiner is a single learned linear layer. It takes a feature vector for each frame composed of three things stacked together: the current ensemble prediction, the frame-to-frame delta, and the squared values. The delta tells the layer how fast something is changing. The squared term gives it nonlinearity. The output is interpolated with the original prediction by a blend factor alpha, which controls how aggressively the corrector is applied. 

def refine(y, alpha=0.4):
    # y: (T, 52) ensemble prediction
    delta = torch.diff(y, dim=0, prepend=y[:1])
    features = torch.cat([y, delta, y ** 2], dim=-1)  # (T, 156)
    correction = features @ W + b                      # (T, 52)
    refined = (1 - alpha) * y + alpha * correction
    return refined.clamp(0, 1)

The refiner is trained separately, on the ensemble's outputs, with MAE on the validation set. It has on the order of a few thousand parameters. It is fast enough that it does not register in the latency budget.

This is, structurally, a learned residual. We are saying: take the ensemble's best guess, then apply a small correction that restores the expressivity the main loss flattened. It works because the corrections are systematic. The model is not random in the directions it underactivates. Brows always lag. Blinks always undershoot. A linear layer can learn that.

Specifically, the refiner pulls up four coefficient families that were chronically underactivated: browDown, eyeBlink, eyeLookDown, and noseSneer. With alpha = 0.4 the validation MAE drops from ~0.0186 to 0.01844, and RMSE from 0.0429 to 0.04289. The numerical improvement is small. The visual improvement is not.

3. The Blink Module

Even after the refiner, blinks were the weak point. The model produced something blink-shaped maybe once every 8 to 10 seconds, and the amplitude was apologetic. Real human blinks happen every 3 to 5 seconds and have crisp opening and closing dynamics.

The reason is simple: blinks are temporally sparse, mostly uncorrelated with the audio, and the dataset just does not have enough of them. No amount of clever loss weighting was going to fix that from the audio signal alone.

We accepted that and added a deterministic blink generator on top. It does not look at the audio. It generates blinks as cosine pulses with these parameters:

  • Inter-blink interval: 2.4 to 4.8 seconds, sampled uniformly.
  • Pulse duration: 110 to 190 milliseconds.
  • Peak amplitude: 0.45 to 0.78.
  • Side coefficients: small proportional bumps to eyeSquint, browDown, and eyeWide for visual coherence.

The generator is seeded deterministically from the input filename, so the same audio clip always gets the same blink pattern. This matters in a competition setting because evaluators expect reproducible outputs. It also matters in product use: you do not want the same character to blink differently every time they say the same line.

The blink module does not improve any quantitative metric we report. It is a presentation layer, not a model improvement. We say so explicitly in the report. The reason it exists is that 0.018 MAE on coefficients you cannot see is not the same as a face that looks alive, and the difference matters when a judge is watching the output run in real time.

Results

All numbers below are on the held-out validation set of 54 clips.

Model MAE RMSE Mouth MAE JawOpen MAE
baseline_full_v1 (TCN) 0.01895 0.04514 0.02303 0.02586
hybrid_bgru_v1 (BiGRU) 0.02036 0.04482 0.02419 0.02597
Ensemble + Refiner 0.01844 0.04289 0.02227 0.02438

The per-blendshape error breakdown is more interesting than the average. The hardest coefficients to predict are mouthLowerDownLeft (MAE 0.0781), mouthLowerDownRight (0.0754), and mouthFunnel (0.0722). These are the high-variance mouth coefficients that carry phoneme-specific information and vary heavily between speakers. The easiest ones, like eyeLookOutLeft (0.00012) or tongueOut (0.000027), are basically always zero in the training data and are trivially predicted.

Knowing this matters. If you ever evaluate a model like this only on aggregate MAE, you are averaging over coefficients that contain almost no information. The aggregate number looks better than the model deserves.

What I Would Do Differently

A few things, in rough order of how much I think they would matter.

Use a self-supervised audio backbone. Log-mel features are fine, but HuBERT or WavLM embeddings would almost certainly capture phoneme-level structure better. The text branch was a partial substitute for this. With a strong audio backbone you might not need the text branch at all.

Pseudo-label the synthetic split. We had 353 synthetic clips with audio but no clean blendshape annotations. We barely used them. A pseudo-labeling loop where we generate targets with the current model, filter by confidence, and add the high-confidence subset back into training would have been a real lever. We just ran out of time.

K-fold validation. A 304/54 split with a single seed is fragile. Five-fold would have given us much more reliable signal for ensemble weight tuning and refiner alpha selection. Some of our smaller decisions were essentially noise-level differences.

A speaker-aware refiner. The current refiner has no notion of which speaker it is correcting. The two speakers in the dataset have noticeably different baseline expressivity. A small per-speaker correction term would probably help.

A strictly causal low-latency mode. The TCN model is already causal, but the BiGRU in the ensemble is not. For a real real-time deployment you would either drop the BiGRU or replace it with a unidirectional variant with a small lookahead buffer. The latency budget for live avatar animation is tight.

Closing

The ensemble, the refiner, and the blink module solve three different problems. The ensemble reduces variance in the predictions. The refiner restores expressivity in the regions the main loss flattened. The blink module fills in a behavior the audio cannot supply. None of them is clever in isolation. The composition is what made the system feel alive on stage.

If there is a single takeaway from this project, it is that the gap between a 0.0185 MAE and a face that looks like it is speaking is much bigger than the gap between 0.0190 and 0.0185. Most of our final-stretch effort went into closing that perceptual gap, not the metric one. The judges, it turned out, agreed.
